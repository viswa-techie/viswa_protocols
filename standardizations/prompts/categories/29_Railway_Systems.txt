HISTORICAL CONTEXT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1830 — Huskisson death at Manchester-Liverpool opening: first railway fatality
1879 — Tay Bridge Disaster (Scotland): 75 killed, triggered structural standards
1952 — Harrow and Wealdstone rail crash (112 dead): triggered AWS (Automatic Warning System)
1988 — Clapham Junction rail crash (35 dead): triggered first systematic railway software safety
1994 — CENELEC TC9X established: EN 50126/128/129 development begins
1996 — ERA (European Railway Agency) predecessor bodies working on ETCS
1999 — Paddington/Ladbroke Grove crash (31 dead): TPWS (Train Protection Warning System)
2000 — EN 50128:2001 first edition (software safety for railway)
2003 — EU First Railway Package: interoperability and ERTMS directive
2005 — ETCS Level 2 first commercial deployment (Switzerland Mattstetten–Rothrist)
2009 — Viareggio rail accident (32 dead): tank wagon safety standards review
2011 — EN 50128:2011 (current major revision)
2013 — Lac-Mégantic disaster (Canada): tanker train runaway, 47 dead
2016 — EN 50126-1:2017 revision (risk-based approach with EN 50129)
2019 — EN 50128 Amendment 1 (2020): clarifies tool qualification
2022 — ERTMS Level 3 moving block: first commercial deployment
2024 — FRMCS (5G for railways) first deployments (Germany DB Netz)
         NIS2 Directive formally makes railways critical infrastructure cybersecurity targets

KEY TECHNICAL CONCEPTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1. EN 50128 Software Categories:
   T1: Tools that generate output verified independently (no qualification needed)
   T2: Tools whose errors could affect safety if not detected (tool validation required)
   T3: Tools whose errors could affect safety and not be detected (full qualification)

2. EN 50129 Generic vs. Specific Product Safety Cases:
   Pre-developed module (PDM): Generic safety case
   Application-specific: Full safety case with TSHA (Technical and Operating Context)
   Evidence: hazard log, FMECA, FTA, safety requirements spec, safety audit

3. ETCS Levels:
   Level 0: No ETCS — national STM or no supervision
   Level 1: Balise-based, point supervision, speed profile sent at each balise
   Level 2: Continuous radio (GSM-R/FRMCS), RBC supervises, no lineside signals needed
   Level 3: Moving block, train integrity assumed, headway based on train length

4. CBTC Architecture:
   ATP (Automatic Train Protection): safety critical, SIL 4
   ATO (Automatic Train Operation): operation efficiency, SIL 2/3
   ATS (Automatic Train Supervision): fleet management, SIL 0/1
   Communication: 802.11 WLAN, private LTE, licensed spectrum

5. RAMS: Quantitative targets per EN 50126:
   MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures): hours of operation
   Availability: (MTBF)/(MTBF + MTTR) — target 99.995% for mainline
   Hazard Rate: failures per hour with safety consequence (SIL-linked)
   THR (Tolerable Hazard Rate): typically 10⁻⁹/hr for SIL 4
