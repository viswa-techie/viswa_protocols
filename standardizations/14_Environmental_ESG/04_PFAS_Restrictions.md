# PFAS Restrictions — Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances

**Topic:** PFAS (forever chemicals) regulatory landscape — EU universal restriction proposal, Stockholm Convention, US EPA roadmap, impact on electronics and manufacturing, substitution strategies  
**Standard:** EU REACH Annex XV Universal PFAS Restriction Proposal (2023); Stockholm Convention (PFOS/PFOA); US EPA PFAS Strategic Roadmap; EU Drinking Water Directive  
**SDO:** ECHA; European Commission; US EPA; UNEP/Stockholm Convention Secretariat  
**Audience:** Chemical compliance engineers, materials engineers, product stewardship professionals, electronics design engineers, sustainability managers  
**Prerequisites:** Basic chemistry (fluorine chemistry; polymers), REACH regulation fundamentals, materials science for electronics

## Chapter 1 — Historical Context & Origin Story

### 1.1 Timeline

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1938 | PTFE (Teflon) accidentally discovered by Roy Plunkett (DuPont) | First fluoropolymer; beginning of PFAS era |
| 1950s | PFOS and PFOA commercial production begins (3M; DuPont) | Used as surfactants, processing aids; extremely stable |
| 1960s-1990s | Widespread industrial use of PFAS in thousands of applications | Waterproofing; non-stick; fire-fighting foam; electronics; semiconductors |
| 1998 | 3M internal studies link PFOS to health effects | First internal industry awareness of toxicity |
| 2000 | 3M voluntarily phases out PFOS production | Landmark decision; triggered global regulatory attention |
| 2004 | US EPA PFOA Stewardship Program | 8 major companies commit to eliminate PFOA emissions by 2015 |
| 2006 | EU PFOS Restriction (2006/122/EC) → later REACH Annex XVII Entry 53 | First EU restriction on a PFAS substance |
| 2009 | **Stockholm Convention adds PFOS** (Annex B) | Global restriction on PFOS production and use |
| 2016 | EU Drinking Water Directive discussion on PFAS limits | Emerging awareness of water contamination |
| 2017 | EU REACH restricts PFOA (Annex XVII Entry 68); effective 2020 | <25 ppb PFOA; <1 mg/kg PFOA-related substances |
| 2019 | **Stockholm Convention adds PFOA** (Annex A) | Global elimination; limited exemptions |
| 2020 | EU Drinking Water Directive recast: 0.1 μg/L total PFAS limit | Extremely strict water quality standard |
| 2021 | Stockholm Convention adds PFHxS | Third PFAS class globally restricted |
| 2023 | **EU Universal PFAS Restriction Proposal** (Annex XV dossier by 5 member states) | Broadest chemical restriction ever proposed: ~10,000 PFAS substances; full ban with limited derogations |
| 2023 | US EPA proposed PFAS drinking water limits (4 ppt for PFOA/PFOS) | Near-zero limits; massive water treatment implications |
| 2024 | ECHA public consultation on universal restriction; RAC/SEAC evaluation begins | Industry comments; economic analysis; exemption discussions |
| 2025 | RAC/SEAC opinions expected; Commission decision anticipated 2025-2026 | Restriction entry in REACH Annex XVII; transition periods 1.5-12+ years |
| 2026-2027 | Expected enforcement start (with transition periods per use) | Electronics industry must begin substitution |

### 1.2 What Are PFAS?

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Definition** | Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances: chemicals with at least one fully fluorinated carbon atom (–CF₂– or –CF₃) |
| **Number** | ~10,000+ individual PFAS substances identified (OECD definition) |
| **Key property** | Carbon-fluorine bond (C–F) is one of strongest in chemistry → extraordinary chemical, thermal, and biological stability |
| **Why "forever chemicals"** | Do not break down in environment under natural conditions; persist for thousands of years; bioaccumulate |
| **Common types** | PFOS (perfluorooctane sulfonate); PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid); PFHxS; GenX; fluoropolymers (PTFE, FEP, PVDF, ETFE); fluoroelastomers (FKM/Viton) |
| **Health concerns** | Cancer (kidney, testicular); thyroid disease; immune system effects; reproductive harm; liver damage; elevated cholesterol |
| **Environmental concerns** | Ubiquitous in water, soil, air; contaminated drinking water globally; bioaccumulation in food chains; found in human blood worldwide |

## Chapter 2 — Standard Architecture & Structure

### 2.1 EU Universal PFAS Restriction Proposal Structure

| Section | Content | Key Points |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Scope** | ALL PFAS (as defined by OECD modified definition) | Any substance with ≥1 fully fluorinated methyl (CF₃–) or methylene (–CF₂–) carbon atom |
| **Proposed restriction** | Ban on manufacture, use, placing on market of PFAS in all applications | With specific derogations/transition periods per use |
| **Transition periods** | 1.5 years (general) to 12+ years (specific critical uses) | Use-specific; based on availability of alternatives |
| **Derogation categories** | Full ban; ban with transition; time-limited derogation; possible "essential use" | Some uses may get longer periods or indefinite derogation |
| **Proposing countries** | Germany, Netherlands, Denmark, Norway, Sweden (joint submission) | Largest restriction proposal in REACH history |
| **Estimated impact** | €billions in substitution costs; affects virtually every industry sector | Electronics, automotive, aerospace, medical, chemicals, textiles, food packaging |

### 2.2 PFAS Classification

| PFAS Type | Structure | Common Members | Typical Applications |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Perfluoroalkyl acids (PFAAs)** | Fully fluorinated carbon chain with functional group | PFOS (C8, sulfonate); PFOA (C8, carboxylate); PFHxA (C6); PFNA (C9) | Historically: surfactants, processing aids; now mostly restricted |
| **Fluoropolymers** | High-molecular-weight polymers with C-F backbone | PTFE (Teflon); FEP; PFA; PVDF; ETFE | Wire insulation; gaskets; coatings; tubing; membrane; semiconductor processing |
| **Fluoroelastomers** | Fluorinated rubber polymers | FKM (Viton®); FFKM (Kalrez®) | O-rings; seals; gaskets; chemical-resistant applications |
| **Fluorinated gases** | Low-MW fluorinated compounds | HFCs; SF₆; NF₃; fluorinated ethers | Semiconductor etching; cleaning; insulating gas (SF₆ in switchgear) |
| **Side-chain fluorinated polymers** | Non-fluorinated backbone with fluorinated side chains | Fluorinated acrylates; fluorinated urethanes | Water/oil/stain repellent coatings; textile treatments |
| **PFAS-containing mixtures** | PFAS as additives in formulations | Fluorosurfactants in coatings; PFAS in fire-fighting foams (AFFF) | Industrial processes; lubricants; coatings; fire suppression |

### 2.3 Current PFAS Restrictions (Already in Force)

| Restriction | Scope | Threshold | Status |
|-------------|-------|:---------:|:------:|
| REACH Entry 53 (PFOS) | Use and placing on market | <10 mg/kg in substances; <1 μg/m² in textiles | In force (2010) |
| REACH Entry 68 (PFOA) | Use and placing on market | <25 ppb PFOA; <1 mg/kg PFOA-related | In force (2020) |
| REACH Entry 68a (PFHxS) | Use and placing on market | Similar to PFOA | In force (2023) |
| REACH Entry 68b (C9-C14 PFCAs) | Use and placing on market | <25 ppb each; <260 ppb sum | In force (2023) |
| Stockholm Convention PFOS | Global production/use ban | Exemptions for specific uses | In force (2009) |
| Stockholm Convention PFOA | Global elimination | Limited exemptions (semiconductors; firefighting foam for specific uses) | In force (2019) |
| Stockholm Convention PFHxS | Global elimination | Limited exemptions | In force (2022) |
| EU Drinking Water Directive | Drinking water quality | **0.1 μg/L total PFAS** (or 0.5 μg/L for sum of 20 specified PFAS) | In force (2023) |
| US EPA NPDWR (proposed) | Drinking water | 4 ppt (ng/L) for PFOA and PFOS individually | Finalized 2024 |

## Chapter 3 — Technical Deep Dive

### 3.1 PFAS in Electronics

| Application | PFAS Type Used | Function | Criticality | Alternatives |
|-------------|:---:|---------|:---:|---|
| **Wire/cable insulation** | PTFE, FEP, ETFE, PFA | High-temperature insulation; low dielectric constant; chemical resistance | **Critical** (no equivalent for high-temp/high-freq) | Silicone (limited temp); PE/PP (lower temp); polyimide (expensive; limited) |
| **O-rings and seals** | FKM (Viton); FFKM (Kalrez) | Chemical resistance; temperature range (-20 to +250°C); fuel resistance | **Critical** (aggressive chemical environments) | EPDM; silicone; NBR (limited chemical resistance); HNBR |
| **Printed circuit board (PCB)** | PTFE laminates (high-frequency) | Low Dk/Df for RF/microwave; signal integrity | **Critical** (5G, radar, aerospace RF) | Hydrocarbon-based low-loss laminates (partial; higher loss) |
| **Semiconductor manufacturing** | PFAS etchants (NF₃, SF₆, C₄F₈); PFAS surfactants; PFAS photoresists | Plasma etching; cleaning; photolithography; CMP slurries | **Critical** (no known alternatives for many processes) | Under intensive R&D; limited alternatives for advanced nodes |
| **Conformal coatings** | Fluorinated coatings (moisture barrier) | Water/oil repellency; corrosion protection on PCBs | Moderate | Parylene; silicone; acrylic; polyurethane coatings |
| **Thermal interface materials (TIM)** | PFAS-containing greases/compounds | Thermal conductivity; gap filling; chemical stability | Moderate | Non-fluorinated greases; phase-change materials; graphite |
| **Connector lubricants** | PTFE dry lubricants; fluorinated greases | Low friction; contact protection; durability | Moderate | MoS₂; graphite; silicone-based lubricants |
| **Solder flux** | Fluorosurfactants in some formulations | Wetting improvement; activity | Low | Non-fluorinated surfactants; reformulated fluxes |
| **Display coatings** | Fluorinated anti-fingerprint coatings | Oleophobic/hydrophobic surface | Low | Silicone-based coatings; ceramic nanocoatings |

### 3.2 Proposed Restriction Transition Periods (Electronics-Relevant)

| Application | Proposed Transition | Rationale | Alternative Status |
|-------------|:---:|---------|:---:|
| Fluoropolymers in semiconductor manufacturing | **12+ years** (longest; possible derogation) | No alternatives for advanced processes; irreplaceable for <7nm node; industry submitted extensive evidence | Active R&D; no viable alternatives at scale |
| PTFE/FEP wire insulation (industrial/safety-critical) | **6.5-12 years** | Safety-critical applications; no alternative meets full performance envelope | Silicone partially viable; not for all conditions |
| FKM seals in industrial equipment | **6.5 years** | Many applications covered by alternatives; critical applications need longer | EPDM, HNBR cover many uses; not all chemical environments |
| PTFE high-frequency PCB laminates | **5-12 years** (debated) | 5G/radar infrastructure; defense; no alternative with equivalent Dk/Df | Hydrocarbon laminates approaching; not equivalent yet |
| Conformal coatings | **1.5-5 years** | Non-fluorinated alternatives exist and are proven | Parylene; silicone; acrylic — widely available |
| Lubricants (general) | **5 years** | Alternatives exist for most applications | Non-fluorinated lubricants; graphite; MoS₂ |
| Fire-fighting foams (AFFF) — electronics-relevant for data centers | **3.5 years** (training/testing); longer for installed systems | Fluorine-free foams available; performance gap for certain fire types | F3 (fluorine-free foams) qualify for many applications |

## Chapter 4 — Implementation Guide

### 4.1 PFAS Compliance Readiness Program

| Phase | Timeline | Actions | Output |
|:-----:|:--------:|---------|--------|
| **1. Inventory** | 0-6 months | Identify ALL PFAS in product portfolio (materials, components, processes); survey suppliers; analytical screening where uncertain | PFAS inventory register (product × material × PFAS type × function × volume) |
| **2. Risk assessment** | 3-9 months | Map each PFAS use to proposed restriction category; determine transition period; assess criticality (can product function without?) | Risk matrix (likelihood of restriction × impact on product) |
| **3. Alternative identification** | 6-18 months | For each PFAS use: research non-PFAS alternatives; evaluate technical performance; identify gaps | Alternative technology roadmap per application |
| **4. Qualification** | 12-36 months | Test alternatives: reliability, performance, safety qualification; accelerated life testing; environmental testing | Qualification reports; comparative performance data |
| **5. Design change** | 18-48 months | Implement alternatives: design changes; process changes; supply chain transitions; documentation updates | Updated products; new specifications; revised BOMs |
| **6. Regulatory engagement** | Ongoing | Participate in public consultations; provide data for derogation requests; join industry consortia; monitor RAC/SEAC opinions | Consultation submissions; industry position papers |
| **7. Derogation preparation** | If needed | For critical uses with no alternatives: prepare detailed technical justification; demonstrate R&D efforts; propose transition timeline | Derogation application dossier |

### 4.2 PFAS Testing Methods

| Method | Detects | Sample Types | Sensitivity | Cost |
|--------|---------|:---:|:---------:|:----:|
| **Total Organic Fluorine (TOF)** — Combustion Ion Chromatography (CIC) | Total fluorine (screens for ANY PFAS) | Solids, liquids | ~1 mg/kg (ppm) | €100-300/sample |
| **Extractable Organic Fluorine (EOF)** | Fluorine in extractable organic fraction | Solids, liquids | ~0.5 mg/kg | €200-400/sample |
| **LC-MS/MS (targeted)** | Specific PFAS compounds (PFOA, PFOS, PFHxS, etc.) | Water, solids, extracts | 0.1-10 ng/L (ppt); 1-10 μg/kg (ppb) | €200-500/sample |
| **GC-MS** | Volatile/semi-volatile PFAS (fluorotelomer alcohols) | Air, solids | 1-50 μg/kg | €200-400/sample |
| **TOP assay** (Total Oxidizable Precursor) | Precursor PFAS that convert to PFAAs upon oxidation | Water, solids | 1-10 ng/L | €300-600/sample |
| **XRF** | Total fluorine (screening; not specific to organic F) | Solids; surfaces | ~100 mg/kg | €20-50/sample |
| **PIGE** (Particle-Induced Gamma Emission) | Total fluorine on surfaces | Surfaces, textiles | ~10 mg/kg surface | Specialized |

**Screening strategy:**
1. XRF or TOF for rapid screening (identifies if fluorine present)
2. If positive: EOF to confirm organic fluorine (PFAS-type)
3. If positive: LC-MS/MS for specific PFAS identification and quantification
4. For compliance with specific limits (PFOA <25 ppb): targeted LC-MS/MS required

### 4.3 Supplier Communication Template

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Request** | "Please confirm whether any materials/components supplied to us contain PFAS (per- or polyfluoroalkyl substances) as defined by [OECD definition / EU restriction proposal definition]" |
| **Definition provided** | "PFAS means any substance containing at least one fully fluorinated methyl (CF₃–) or methylene (–CF₂–) carbon atom (without any H/Cl/Br/I attached to it)" |
| **Examples** | "This includes but is not limited to: PTFE, FEP, PFA, PVDF, ETFE (fluoropolymers); FKM/Viton, FFKM/Kalrez (fluoroelastomers); fluorinated surfactants; PFOS; PFOA; GenX; fluorinated coatings; fluorinated lubricants" |
| **Requested information** | (1) Does the supplied material/component contain any PFAS? (Yes/No/Unknown). (2) If yes: which PFAS? (substance name/CAS if known). (3) Function of PFAS in the material. (4) Concentration (% or mg/kg if known). (5) Can you supply a PFAS-free alternative? |
| **Deadline** | "Please respond within [30/60] days" |
| **Consequence** | "This information is needed to assess our obligations under [current and upcoming PFAS restrictions]. Inability to confirm PFAS-free status may affect future procurement decisions." |

## Chapter 5 — Regulatory Compliance

### 5.1 Current Compliance Requirements (Already Enforceable)

| Requirement | Trigger | Action | Evidence |
|-------------|---------|--------|----------|
| PFOS (REACH Entry 53) | Any product containing PFOS ≥10 mg/kg | Cannot place on EU market (already banned) | Material declarations; testing |
| PFOA (REACH Entry 68) | Product contains PFOA ≥25 ppb OR PFOA-related ≥1 mg/kg | Cannot place on EU market (limited exemptions until 2025/2030) | LC-MS/MS analysis; supplier declarations |
| PFHxS (REACH Entry 68a) | Product contains PFHxS above threshold | Cannot place on EU market | Testing; declarations |
| C9-C14 PFCAs (REACH Entry 68b) | Product contains long-chain PFCAs above thresholds | Cannot place on EU market | Testing |
| Stockholm Convention (national implementation) | Production/use of PFOS, PFOA, PFHxS | Prohibited (limited exemptions) | Compliance programs |
| REACH SVHC (candidate list PFAS) | PFOA salts, GenX (HFPO-DA), and others on candidate list at >0.1% in article | Article 33 communication; SCIP; possible Art 7(2) notification | Material data; SVHC assessment |

### 5.2 Future Requirements (Universal Restriction — Preparing Now)

| Action | Priority | When |
|--------|:--------:|------|
| Complete PFAS inventory across product portfolio | **HIGH** | Now (before restriction enters force) |
| Identify critical PFAS uses with no alternatives | **HIGH** | Now (needed for derogation justification) |
| Engage with industry consortia on derogations | High | Now (public consultation participation) |
| Begin qualification of alternatives (non-critical uses) | High | Now-12 months |
| Establish supplier communication program for PFAS | High | Now |
| Budget for substitution R&D (critical uses) | Medium | 12-24 months |
| Implement design changes for non-critical PFAS uses | Medium | 12-36 months (before transition period ends) |
| Prepare derogation applications (if needed for critical uses) | Medium | 18-36 months (before restriction enforcement) |
| Full compliance (all non-derogated uses PFAS-free) | Required | By end of transition period (use-specific) |

## Chapter 6 — Regional Context

### 6.1 PFAS Regulations by Region

| Region | Regulation | Scope | Status |
|--------|-----------|-------|:------:|
| **EU** | Universal PFAS restriction (REACH Annex XVII proposed) | ALL PFAS (~10,000 substances); all uses | Proposed (2023); expected adoption 2025-2026; enforcement 2026-2028+ with transitions |
| **EU** | PFOS/PFOA/PFHxS/C9-C14 restrictions (already in force) | Specific PFAS already restricted | In force |
| **EU** | Drinking Water Directive | 0.1 μg/L total PFAS | In force (2023) |
| **US (Federal)** | EPA PFAS Strategic Roadmap; NPDWR (drinking water); TSCA SNURs | Drinking water: 4 ppt PFOA/PFOS; TSCA reporting for all PFAS manufacturers | Drinking water: finalized 2024; TSCA reporting: 2025 |
| **US (States)** | State-level PFAS bans (ME, MN, CA, WA, NY, etc.) | Varies: food packaging; textiles; firefighting foam; "all products" (Maine 2030) | Various; Maine most comprehensive (ban all intentional PFAS by 2030) |
| **Canada** | PFOS/PFOA prohibited; PFAS class approach under development | Prohibition; environmental monitoring | PFOS/PFOA in force; broader approach developing |
| **Japan** | Chemical Substances Control Law; PFOS/PFOA restricted | Specific PFAS; industrial controls | In force for PFOS/PFOA |
| **Australia** | National PFAS guidance; state regulations; PFAS NEMP | Focus on contaminated sites; industrial discharge | Guidance-based; tightening |
| **Global** | Stockholm Convention (PFOS, PFOA, PFHxS) | Production and use elimination globally | In force (with exemptions) |

### 6.2 EU Universal Restriction vs. US Approach

| Aspect | EU (Universal PFAS Restriction) | US (Federal + State Patchwork) |
|--------|:---:|:---:|
| Approach | **Class-based** (all PFAS restricted as a group) | **Substance-by-substance** (federal) + class-based (some states) |
| Scope | All PFAS (~10,000); all uses; all products | Federal: drinking water + TSCA reporting; States: varies (products; water; foam) |
| Definition | OECD-aligned (≥1 fully fluorinated C atom) | Varies by regulation/state |
| Timeline | Single regulation; use-specific transition periods | Rolling; fragmented; different dates per state/federal rule |
| Exemptions/derogations | Use-specific; must justify "essential use" | Federal: limited; States: varies widely |
| Enforcement | REACH enforcement (market surveillance; ECHA + member states) | EPA + state enforcement; litigation-driven (Superfund; CERCLA) |
| Industry impact | Predictable (single framework); comprehensive; planned | Unpredictable; compliance with multiple jurisdictions; litigation risk |
| Focus | Proactive prevention (stop all PFAS use) | Mixed: remediation (cleanup) + prevention (drinking water + product bans) |

## Chapter 7 — Comparison

### 7.1 PFAS Alternatives Performance Comparison (Electronics)

| Application | Current PFAS | Best Alternative | Performance Gap | Maturity |
|-------------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| High-temp wire insulation (250°C+) | PTFE/FEP (Dk 2.1; Df 0.0002; 260°C) | Silicone (200°C; higher Dk 3.0) | **Significant** (temp + electrical) | Alternative mature but lower performance |
| RF/microwave PCB (>6 GHz) | PTFE laminate (Dk 2.2; Df 0.0009) | Hydrocarbon (Dk 3.0-3.5; Df 0.002) | **Moderate-Large** (higher loss at >20 GHz) | Rapidly improving; gap closing for <20 GHz |
| Chemical-resistant seals (acids/fuels) | FKM/FFKM (-20 to +250°C; broad chemical resistance) | HNBR; EPDM; Silicone | **Moderate** (narrower chemical resistance) | Case-by-case; many applications coverable |
| Semiconductor etch gas | C₄F₈; SF₆; NF₃ | Non-fluorinated plasma alternatives | **Very large** (no equivalent process chemistry for advanced nodes) | Early R&D; decades from production |
| Conformal coating (moisture barrier) | Fluorinated coating | Parylene; silicone; acrylic | **Minimal** (most applications covered) | Mature; widely deployed |
| Connector lubricant | PTFE dry lubricant | MoS₂; graphite; silicone oil | **Small** (most applications) | Mature alternatives; some high-temp gaps |
| Anti-fingerprint coating (displays) | Fluorosilane SAM coating | Silicone-based oleophobic | **Small** (durability slightly lower) | Commercially available |
| Fire-fighting foam (data centers) | AFFF (fluorinated) | F3 (fluorine-free foam) | **Closing** (Class B fire; hydrocarbons) | Commercial; some performance gaps for polar solvents |

### 7.2 PFAS Regulation vs. Other Substance Restrictions

| Dimension | PFAS Universal Restriction | RoHS | REACH SVHC | Asbestos Ban |
|-----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Number of substances | ~10,000 (class-based) | 10 | 240+ (individual assessment) | ~6 mineral types |
| Approach | Class restriction (structural definition) | Individual substance listing | Individual SVHC identification | Class ban (all forms) |
| Industry impact | €billions; affects all sectors | €billions (electronics only) | Varies per substance | Major (construction; industrial) |
| Alternatives availability | Varies: some available; some no alternative exists | Generally available (after 15+ years) | Varies | Available for most uses |
| Transition approach | Use-specific transition periods (1.5-12+ years) | Exemptions (time-limited; renewable) | Authorization + sunset dates | Bans with limited legacy exemptions |
| Historical parallel | Most comparable to asbestos (ubiquitous; class ban) | Substance-specific | Chemical-by-chemical | Class ban precedent |

    Note over ECHA,IND: 2025-2026: Decision
    ECHA->>COM: RAC + SEAC opinions transmitted
    COM->>COM: Draft restriction entry<br/>(Annex XVII amendment)
    COM->>COM: Comitology procedure<br/>(member state vote)
    COM->>ECHA: Restriction adopted<br/>(published in OJ)
    
    Note over ECHA,IND: 2026-2028+: Enforcement
    ECHA->>IND: Restriction in force<br/>(transition periods begin)
    IND->>IND: Substitute (per transition period)

### 9.1 Case Study: Electronics Manufacturer PFAS Inventory & Substitution

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Company | Industrial electronics manufacturer; 300 product families (control systems, sensors, power electronics); aerospace and automotive qualified |
| PFAS inventory results | Total PFAS-containing materials identified: 47 distinct materials across product portfolio. Breakdown: (1) FKM O-rings/gaskets: 23 part numbers (most common; in IP67 enclosures). (2) PTFE wire insulation: 8 cable types (high-temp applications >150°C). (3) PTFE/FEP tubing: 3 types (chemical environments). (4) PTFE PCB laminates: 2 types (RF products; 2.4GHz and 5.8GHz designs). (5) Fluorinated conformal coating: 1 type (moisture protection on 40+ products). (6) FKM connector seals: 5 types (harsh environment connectors). (7) PFAS-containing lubricant: 2 types (mechanism lubrication). (8) Fluorosurfactant in solder flux: 1 formulation. |
| Criticality assessment | **Critical (no alternative today)**: PTFE wire for applications >200°C (4 cable types used in exhaust-adjacent automotive ECUs); PTFE laminate for 5.8GHz radar products. **Substitutable with effort**: FKM O-rings (most applications <150°C; silicone/EPDM viable); PTFE wire for 150-200°C (silicone wire viable); 2.4GHz PCB laminates (hydrocarbon Rogers RO4000 viable). **Easy substitution**: conformal coating (switch to silicone-based); lubricants (silicone or MoS₂); solder flux (non-fluorinated formulation available from supplier). |
| Action plan | **Phase 1 (0-18 months)**: Substitute easy wins: conformal coating → silicone (qualification: 6 months; 40 products affected); lubricant → MoS₂/silicone (qualification: 3 months; 15 products); solder flux → non-fluorinated (supplier qualification: 4 months). **Phase 2 (12-36 months)**: Substitute medium-effort: FKM O-rings → silicone/EPDM for <150°C applications (extensive testing: temperature, IP rating, accelerated aging; 23 part numbers; ~18 months qualification); PTFE wire (150-200°C) → silicone-insulated (UL qualification required; ~12 months). 2.4GHz PCB → Rogers RO4003C hydrocarbon laminate (RF characterization + EMC re-certification; ~12 months). **Phase 3 (24-48 months)**: Address critical uses: PTFE wire >200°C: evaluate high-temp polyimide + silicone hybrid; may need product redesign to reduce thermal exposure; if no alternative: prepare derogation justification. PTFE 5.8GHz radar laminate: evaluate emerging low-loss hydrocarbon laminates; if insufficient: derogation. |
| Cost estimate | Phase 1: ~€200K (qualification + documentation). Phase 2: ~€1.2M (extensive qualification + re-certification + supplier development). Phase 3: ~€500K (R&D + derogation preparation) or significantly more if product redesign needed. Total: ~€2M over 4 years. |
| Lessons | (1) Start now — don't wait for restriction enforcement. (2) FKM O-rings are the most common PFAS in electronics (but often substitutable). (3) Critical uses are typically <10% of PFAS applications but 50%+ of the compliance challenge. (4) Supplier engagement essential (many alternatives already exist; suppliers can reformulate). (5) Qualification is the bottleneck, not availability of alternatives. |

### 9.2 Case Study: Semiconductor Industry PFAS Derogation Strategy

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Industry | Semiconductor manufacturing (wafer fabrication); global industry; €500B+ revenue |
| PFAS uses in semiconductor fab | (1) **Plasma etch gases**: CF₄, C₂F₆, C₃F₈, C₄F₈, SF₆, NF₃ — essential for patterning silicon, oxides, nitrides. (2) **Photolithography**: fluorinated photoresist components; PFAS in immersion lithography fluid; PFAS surfactants in resist coatings. (3) **Chemical Mechanical Planarization (CMP)**: fluorinated surfactants in slurries. (4) **Cleaning**: fluorinated solvents/surfactants for wafer cleaning. (5) **Fluoropolymer components**: PTFE/PFA piping, tubing, gaskets, liners (ultra-pure chemical delivery; contamination prevention). |
| Why derogation needed | No known alternatives for many processes at advanced technology nodes (≤7nm). Alternative etch chemistries do not achieve required selectivity/anisotropy. Fluoropolymer piping irreplaceable for ultra-pure chemical delivery (metallic contamination from alternatives would destroy yields). Industry estimates 5-10+ years minimum R&D timeline for viable alternatives for some processes. |
| Industry response | (1) **WSC (World Semiconductor Council)** and **SEMI** (industry association) coordinated global response to EU consultation. (2) Submitted detailed technical evidence: process-specific justification for each PFAS use; alternative assessment; R&D roadmaps. (3) Proposed: 12+ year transition period for semiconductor-specific uses; regular review with progress reporting; commitment to R&D investment. (4) **PFC abatement**: highlighted industry's investment in exhaust gas abatement systems (>99% destruction of fluorinated etch gases before atmospheric release); distinguished between PFAS "use" and PFAS "emission." |
| Current status | Strong expectation of semiconductor-specific derogation (12+ years); SEAC acknowledging socioeconomic arguments; RAC evaluating risk justification; Commission likely to grant longest transition for semiconductor uses. |

## Chapter 11 — Interview Questions & Career Guide

### Tier 1: Entry-Level

**Q1:** What are PFAS and why are they called "forever chemicals"?  
**A:** PFAS = Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances — a class of ~10,000 synthetic chemicals containing carbon-fluorine (C-F) bonds. They're called "forever chemicals" because the C-F bond is one of the strongest in organic chemistry (bond energy ~485 kJ/mol), making PFAS extraordinarily resistant to degradation. They don't break down in the environment (water, soil, air) under any natural conditions — they persist essentially indefinitely. This creates: bioaccumulation in organisms and food chains; global contamination of drinking water; presence in human blood worldwide. Health effects include cancer, thyroid disease, immune dysfunction, and reproductive harm. Examples in electronics: PTFE (Teflon) wire insulation; FKM (Viton) O-rings; fluorinated coatings; semiconductor etch gases.

**Q2:** What PFAS are already restricted under EU REACH?  
**A:** Currently restricted (Annex XVII): (1) **PFOS** (Entry 53): <10 mg/kg in substances; <1 μg/m² in textiles; in force since 2010. (2) **PFOA** (Entry 68): <25 ppb (25 μg/kg) PFOA; <1 mg/kg PFOA-related substances; in force since 2020; some exemptions until 2025/2030. (3) **PFHxS** (Entry 68a): restricted similarly; in force since 2023. (4) **C9-C14 PFCAs** (Entry 68b): <25 ppb individual; <260 ppb sum; in force 2023. Additionally, several PFAS are on the SVHC candidate list (PFOA salts; GenX/HFPO-DA; PFBS), triggering Article 33 communication duties if >0.1% in articles. The proposed **universal PFAS restriction** would cover ALL remaining ~10,000 PFAS substances — this is expected to enter force around 2026-2028 with use-specific transition periods.

### Tier 2: Mid-Level

**Q3:** How would you assess a product portfolio for PFAS exposure and prioritize substitution?  
**A:** [Detailed answer covering: (1) Systematic inventory approach: material type screening (any material described as "fluorinated," "Teflon," "Viton," "PTFE," "FEP," "PVDF," etc.); supplier questionnaire with PFAS definition; analytical screening (TOF for total organic fluorine) for uncertain materials. (2) Categorization: by PFAS type (fluoropolymer vs. fluoroelastomer vs. PFAS additive); by function (insulation; sealing; coating; processing aid); by criticality (performance gap with alternatives). (3) Prioritization matrix: plot each PFAS use by (X: alternative readiness) vs. (Y: transition period expected). Quadrant 1 (short transition + alternative ready): immediate action. Quadrant 2 (short transition + no alternative): urgent R&D + derogation prep. Quadrant 3 (long transition + alternative ready): planned transition. Quadrant 4 (long transition + no alternative): monitor + R&D. (4) Action: substitute easy wins first (conformal coatings, lubricants); then medium-effort (O-rings, moderate-temp wires); then critical (high-temp, semiconductor). Budget and timeline per quadrant.]

### Tier 3: Senior

**Q5:** Your company relies heavily on fluoropolymers in safety-critical aerospace electronics. The EU universal PFAS restriction is adopted. Design your regulatory and technical response strategy.  
**A:** [Comprehensive answer covering: (1) Regulatory strategy: participate in public consultation with detailed technical evidence; join industry consortium (ASD-STAN for aerospace); prepare derogation applications per REACH Article 68(2) for aerospace-critical uses; provide evidence of no available alternatives meeting safety/performance requirements (DO-160G environmental qualification; component reliability data). (2) Technical roadmap: map each fluoropolymer use to specific performance requirements (temperature; dielectric; chemical resistance; outgassing); evaluate alternatives against actual spec requirements (not just general properties); initiate R&D for custom alternatives where gap exists; partner with material suppliers on development programs. (3) Transition planning: products with non-critical PFAS uses → substitute within transition period; products with critical uses → operate under derogation; develop next-generation products designed PFAS-free from start. (4) Supply chain: engage fluoropolymer suppliers on their strategy (will they continue manufacturing under derogation?); qualify alternative materials; dual-source during transition. (5) Certification impact: any material change in aerospace requires re-certification (DO-160G; RTCA/DO-254); factor 2-3 year certification cycles into transition planning; coordinate with certifying authorities on approach.]

## Chapter 12 — Cheat Sheet & Quick Reference

### PFAS Definition (Restriction Scope)

```
EU Universal Restriction Definition (modified OECD):
  ANY substance containing at least ONE fully fluorinated methyl (CF₃–)
  or methylene (–CF₂–) carbon atom (without any H/Cl/Br/I attached to it)

This covers:
  ✓ Fluoropolymers (PTFE, FEP, PFA, PVDF, ETFE)
  ✓ Fluoroelastomers (FKM, FFKM)
  ✓ Perfluoroalkyl acids (PFOA, PFOS, PFHxA, PFNA)
  ✓ Fluorotelomers (precursors)
  ✓ Fluorinated gases (SF₆, NF₃, HFCs, C₄F₈)
  ✓ Side-chain fluorinated polymers
  ✓ Fluorosurfactants
  ✓ ANY substance matching the structural definition (~10,000 substances)
```
